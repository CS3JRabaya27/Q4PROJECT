What is Nuclear Fission?

Introduction to Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission is a process where the nucleus of an atom splits into smaller parts, releasing a large amount of energy. This phenomenon is the principle behind both nuclear reactors that generate electricity and the powerful explosions of atomic bombs. Understanding nuclear fission is crucial for grasping how we harness nuclear energy and manage its risks.

The Science Behind Nuclear Fission

At the core of nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy atomic nucleus, typically uranium-235 or plutonium-239, when it absorbs a neutron. This split produces two smaller nuclei, additional neutrons, and a considerable amount of energy in the form of heat and radiation. The extra neutrons can induce further fission reactions, creating a chain reaction that can be controlled in a nuclear reactor or unleashed in a nuclear bomb.

Applications of Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission is predominantly used in nuclear power plants to generate electricity. Here, the heat from fission reactions is used to produce steam that drives turbines, generating electrical power. In the military sector, fission is the fundamental process behind the explosive power of atomic bombs. Additionally, the medical field uses isotopes produced by fission reactions for diagnostic imaging and cancer treatments.
